Ward-level view of Stations Sized by Total Flow

Note: This map visualizes MBTA subway stations in Boston, with markers sized by passenger flow and colored by subway line (black indicating transfer stations serving multiple lines), overlaid on Boston's ward boundaries. Click the station markers to see the station name and amount of rider flow. Hover over the map to see Neighborhood Information.

About This Visualization

This interactive map illustrates how ridership flow patterns vary across Boston's neighborhoods, highlighting areas with the most significant differences. By identifying these neighborhood-level disparities, the visualization reveals potential opportunities to optimize services, enabling targeted adjustments that align closely with actual community needs and behaviors.

Key Insights

Central Boston Transfer Stations

The stations with the highest average daily flow—Park Street, Downtown Crossing, South Station, and North Station—are concentrated within Central Boston, highlighting the area as a major hub for employment, tourism, and transit connectivity. Three of these busiest stations are notable transfer points between subway lines, which significantly boosts foot traffic. Although South Station only services the MBTA Red Line in this visualization, its broader role as a key intermodal hub connecting the subway to commuter rail systems likely contributes heavily to its high passenger volume. This underscores how transportation infrastructure and urban geography converge in Central Boston, emphasizing network connectivity as a primary driver of ridership. Future analyses incorporating commuter rail and bus networks could further clarify transit flow dynamics and more accurately reflect the importance of intermodal connections.

Geographical Disparities

Outside of Central Boston, stations with moderate to high passenger flows are concentrated in neighborhoods like Fenway/Kenmore, Back Bay, East Boston, Charlestown, Jamaica Plain/Mission Hill, South End, and Dorchester, reflecting their residential density, educational institutions, and local economic activity. Surrounding communities, including Brookline, Newton, Cambridge, and Malden, also show substantial transit usage, highlighting their integration into Boston's broader commuting network. However, significant disparities in subway accessibility become evident in areas like Roxbury, Hyde Park, Roslindale/Moss Hill/West Roxbury, Seaport, Mattapan, North Dorchester, and Allston/Brighton, where subway coverage is sparse or nonexistent. For instance, the rapidly developing Seaport District relies on the Silver Line bus service rather than direct subway access due to geographical and financial constraints, limiting capacity and convenience. Similarly, Mattapan's dependence on the Mattapan Trolley—a light rail rather than a full subway service—reflects historical infrastructure investment decisions that continue to impact mobility, economic opportunity, and growth. Addressing these inequities through targeted planning and investment is crucial to achieving a more equitable transit future and enhancing connectivity for underserved neighborhoods. See this link for more information about Journey-Based Transit Equity in the Greater Boston Area.

Implications

This ward-level visualization provides powerful insights into how subway usage varies across neighborhoods, helping city planners, transit authorities, and community leaders identify where improvements are most needed.

  • High-flow transfer stations in Central Boston underscore the need to maintain strong service frequencies and consider further enhancements to support intermodal connections.
  • Areas with limited or no subway access—like Mattapan, West Roxbury, and parts of the Seaport—may benefit from alternative solutions such as shuttle services, BRT (Bus Rapid Transit), or new rail extensions.
  • Equity-focused planning can be guided by this map, supporting efforts to close the accessibility gap between transit-rich and underserved areas.
  • This view can inform targeted investment strategies, such as upgrading infrastructure in high-traffic zones and improving connectivity in overlooked neighborhoods to support more inclusive transit growth.
