This map visualizes MBTA subway stations in Boston, with markers sized by passenger flow and colored by subway line, overlaid on Boston's ward boundaries.

Central Boston Transfer Stations

The stations with the highest average daily flow—Park Street, Downtown Crossing, South Station, and North Station—are all located within the "Central Boston" area. This concentration of high-traffic stations suggests that Central Boston serves as a major hub for both employment and tourism. Notably, three of the four busiest stations are marked in black, indicating they are transfer points between multiple subway lines, which naturally drives higher foot traffic. While South Station is not shown as a transfer station in this visualization—since it only services the MBTA Red Line—it’s important to recognize its broader role as a key intermodal hub. South Station connects the Red Line with the MBTA Commuter Rail system, which falls outside the scope of the subway dataset used, but likely contributes significantly to its high flow. This visualization highlights how transportation infrastructure and urban geography intersect in Central Boston. Its dominance in station traffic underscores the area's economic and cultural significance. The prominence of transfer stations among the busiest stops suggests that network connectivity is a key driver of ridership. Additionally, the exclusion of intermodal transit connections, like those at South Station, may lead to underestimating the true volume and importance of certain locations. Future analyses that integrate commuter rail and bus networks could provide a more complete picture of transit flow across the city.

Transfer Stations

Outside of Central Boston, stations with moderate to high flow are primarily located in wards like Fenway/Kenmore, Back Bay, East Boston, Charlestown, Jamaica Plain/Mission Hill, South End, and Dorchester. These neighborhoods, while not the commercial core, still host a mix of residential density, educational institutions, and local economic activity, supporting regular ridership. Surrounding towns such as Brookline, Newton, Cambridge, and Malden also demonstrate notable transit usage, reflecting their integration into the greater Boston commuting network. However, a clear disparity in MBTA subway accessibility emerges across several Boston wards. Areas like Roxbury, Hyde Park, Roslindale/Moss Hill/West Roxbury, Seaport, Mattapan, North Dorchester, and Allston/Brighton show sparse or no subway service. This uneven distribution points to broader geographical, economic, and social inequities. For example, the Seaport District—despite its rapid development into a business and residential hub—lacks direct subway access due to its location on reclaimed land and the prohibitive cost of extending existing lines. As a result, it relies on the Silver Line, a bus rapid transit service that offers limited capacity and convenience compared to subway infrastructure. Similarly, Mattapan is served by the Mattapan Trolley, a light rail line rather than a full subway connection. Historical decisions around infrastructure costs and priorities have contributed to limited service in this area. These examples highlight how infrastructural investment—or the lack thereof—can shape mobility, economic opportunity, and urban growth in different neighborhoods. A more equitable transit future would require deliberate planning and investment to better connect historically underserved communities to the city’s core.
