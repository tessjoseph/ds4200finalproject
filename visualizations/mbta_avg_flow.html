MBTA Station Activity Heatmap

Note: This interactive heatmap visualizes ridership patterns across MBTA stations during different time periods. Explore the data by interacting with the map.

About This Visualization

This heatmap shows the average daily passenger flow for each MBTA station across different time periods. The visualization helps identify which stations experience the highest activity levels and when these peak periods occur.

Key Insights

High-Traffic Stations

Downtown Crossing, Park Street, and South Station consistently show the highest activity levels across all time periods, reflecting their status as major transfer points and central locations. meaning that congestion at these locations could impact the overall efficiency of the transit system. Also, stations like Maverick, Airport, Arlington, and Kendall/MIT showed high ridership trends. While not being major transfer stations, these stations may be major tourism and business hubs, and should be prioritized as well when considering major funding reallocations and renovations.

Temporal Patterns

Again, most stations show clear peaks during AM_PEAK and PM_PEAK hours, with significantly reduced activity during early morning and late night periods. This pattern is especially pronounced at stations near business districts like Charles/MGH in the West End with many different medical offices, and Tufts Medical Center near Chinatown. This pattern was expected, as areas with offices attract the most commuters.

Line Comparison

We can also look at differences between the individual lines: Red Line stations generally show higher average flow compared to other lines, particularly at key Cambridge locations like Harvard, Porter, and Davis (more walkable areas with a very significant student population. On the other hand, the Green Line stations display lower, more evenly distributed ridership across their routes (excluding the combined-branch central Boston stations). This pattern may be explained by the fact that Green line branches typically run through residential/suburban towns and neighborhoods like Newton and Brookline. These are more affluent towns where residents may choose to commute by car rather than public transit.

Implications

Understanding this station-specific activity patterns may allow transit planners to prioritize capacity improvements where they're most needed and adjust service schedules to better align with actual usage patterns.

  • Using our data, the MBTA may better understand which stations experience Higher-traffic, and which stations would benefit from expanded platforms and improved passenger flow systems
  • Stations with dramatic peaks could also be candidates for express service during rush hours, skipping over under-utilized stations.
  • Transit planners may also be able to understand neighborhood-specific patterns, which can inform targeted service improvements - such as providing incentives for people living in residential/suburban neighborhoods to choose public transit.
