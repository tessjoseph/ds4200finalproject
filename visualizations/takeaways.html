Key Takeaways from MBTA Ridership Analysis

Note: These insights represent the most significant findings from our analysis of MBTA ridership data across multiple visualizations.

1. MBTA Ridership is Highly Dependent on Work and School Commutes

The line plot and heatmap both reveal that ridership is concentrated around morning (AM_PEAK) and evening (PM_PEAK) hours. This pattern strongly suggests that the MBTA primarily serves >commuters traveling to work and school.

Recommendation: To improve service, the MBTA could reallocate resources by increasing train frequency during peak hours and reducing underused services during the early morning, late night, midday and weekends. From a financial perspective, it may make sense to charge more for peak hour tickets, and charge less for off-peak hour tickets to encourage stable ridership trends throughout the day

2. Major Transfer Stations Experience Consistently High Traffic

The interactive heatmap shows that stations like Downtown Crossing, Park Street, and North Station have continuously high passenger flow across all time periods. These stations act as crucial transfer points, meaning that congestion at these locations could impact the overall efficiency of the transit system. Also, stations like Maverick, Airport, Arlington, and Kendall/MIT showed high ridership trends. While not being major transfer stations, these stations may be major tourism and business hubs, and should be prioritized as well when considering major funding reallocations and renovations.

Recommendation: To reduce congestion, MBTA could consider infrastructure improvements such as expanded platforms and better crowd flow management inside stations, or express trains that bypass low-traffic areas (Ex. NYC Subway, or The London Underground.

3. The Green Line Operates Differently from Other Subway Lines

Unlike other MBTA subway lines, the Green Line branches have more evenly distributed ridership outside the downtown core. This pattern suggests that the Green Line branches (towards Brookline/Netwon) primarily serve residential and suburban areas, rather than high-density business districts.

Recommendation: MBTA planners could explore increasing service frequency on Green Line branches during non-peak hours to support more flexible commuting and local travel. It may also be worth looking into expanding green line service towards farther suburbs, connecting Boston to more high density commuter neighborhoods. This idea was suggested in a recent Transit Supported Density Report from January 2025, suggesting a green line extension towards Needham, MA. [Learn more].

Conclusion

The findings from this project highlight critical ridership trends that can inform service optimizations. By focusing on peak-hour efficiency, infrastructure improvements, and Green Line accessibilit, the MBTA can enhance overall commuter experience and system efficiency.

To further our analysis, next steps could include looking at income-level data thorughout Boston, looking for any disparities in transit access between lower-income and higher-income residential neighborhoods. In addition, we could add other methods of MBTA transit to our analysis, such as buses, ferry, and the commuter rail/silver lines.
